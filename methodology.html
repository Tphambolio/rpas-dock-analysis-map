RPAS Dock Placement Methodology

Edmonton Fire Rescue Services · DJI M4TD Dock Network Analysis v3.0 · April 2026

1. Introduction

Edmonton Fire Rescue Services operates twelve DJI Matrice 4TD (M4TD) drone-in-a-box docks across the city. Each dock provides fully autonomous launch, flight, and landing capabilities, enabling drones to respond to fire incidents without on-site pilot deployment. This analysis determines the optimal placement for a fixed 11-dock network to maximise weighted response coverage across all call types, with particular emphasis on wildland fire (WF) risk in the river valley and ravine system.

The analysis compares three configurations: the current 11-dock deployment, a fully optimised Option B configuration, and Option C — a hybrid that locks in the six existing deployed docks and uses greedy optimisation to select the five best additional locations from the remaining candidates.

Key result: Option C achieves 89.4% wildland fire T1 coverage (≤5 min response) — 2.5 points higher than Option B — while maintaining 97.8% total T1+T2 coverage. This makes it the recommended path for EFRS operational planning.

3. Demand Analysis

Wildland fire incidents cluster along the North Saskatchewan River valley and its tributary ravines (Mill Creek, Whitemud Creek, Beverly ravines, Terwillegar / Oleskiw). This spatial concentration drives the placement of both dock stations and the early detection network in and around the ravine system.

4. Optimization Methodology

Response Time Model

The M4TD achieves 90 seconds from dock alarm to airborne, then cruises at 15 m/s (54 km/h). Coverage tiers are calculated from dock centroid to incident centroid in straight-line distance:

TierMax DistanceMax ResponseSignificance
T13,150 m<5 minOn-scene before first ground unit for most calls
T25,850 m≤8 minConcurrent with first ground unit arrival
T39,450 m≤12 minUseful for prolonged incidents, multi-alarm

Greedy Set-Cover Algorithm

Candidate sites are all 31 City of Edmonton fire stations. The algorithm iterates:

  1. Score each candidate: sum of weighted demand for incidents newly covered at T1, T2, T3 (weights 3, 2, 1)
  2. Select the candidate with the highest marginal score
  3. Mark all newly covered incidents; repeat until 11 docks selected

Option C constrains the first six selections to the existing deployed stations (FS01, FS03, FS13, FS18, FS21, FS31) before running the greedy phase for positions 7–11.

6. PTZ Camera Station Methodology

IQ FireWatch panoramic cameras are proposed at eight elevated sites around the river valley and ravine system. Each station provides 360° optical-NIR-SWIR coverage (400–1,100 nm) with a 15–20 km reliable detection radius. Cameras complete a full panorama in under 6 minutes and are AI-assisted for smoke discrimination against cloud, haze, and dust clutter. Units are rated to −45 °C — suitable for Edmonton winters.

Site selection prioritises: (1) line-of-sight to the highest WF incident density polygons, (2) existing elevated infrastructure (cell towers, hydro transmission structures, rooftops), and (3) complementary overlapping coverage to eliminate ravine shadows.

7. Gas / Particulate Sensor Network

Detection Physics

Wildland fire smoke generates a characteristic CO + PM2.5 co-spike before visible smoke plumes rise above the ravine rim. Installing sensors at ravine floor level captures this early chemical signature — typically 3–8 minutes before PTZ cameras can visually confirm a smoke column from above. Floor sensors trigger an alert; rim sensors cross-validate and provide gradient data to estimate fire intensity and movement direction.

Platform: N5 Sensor Node

The N5 multi-gas sensor (validated under DHS S&T) detects CO, PM2.5, PM10, CO2, O3, temperature, and relative humidity simultaneously. LoRaWAN mesh radios relay data to gateways co-located at fire stations, maintaining <5 min alert latency even without cellular coverage in ravine bottoms. Solar panels with LiFePO4 batteries provide year-round power; the LiFePO4 chemistry retains >85% capacity at −40 °C — suitable for Alberta winters.

8. Integration Architecture

The three systems — gas/particulate sensors, PTZ cameras, and RPAS docks — form a layered detection-to-dispatch pipeline:

LAYER 1 — Chemical Detection (T+0 to T+5 min)
  [Ravine floor sensors]  CO + PM2.5 threshold exceeded
         │
         ▼  LoRaWAN mesh → Fire station gateway → EFRS SCADA
  Alert generated: candidate fire location, intensity estimate

LAYER 2 — Visual Confirmation (T+2 to T+8 min)
  [PTZ cameras]  AI smoke discrimination → operator verify
         │
         ▼  Ethernet / LTE → FireWatch cloud → EFRS ECC
  Smoke confirmed: bearing, spread direction, size class

LAYER 3 — RPAS Dispatch (T+3 to T+12 min, dock-dependent)
  [Nearest dock within T1/T2 zone]
         │
         ▼  DJI FlightHub 2 → autonomous dock launch
  M4TD on-scene: thermal + RGB livestream to EFRS ECC
  Ground units re-routed or pre-positioned based on aerial SA

LAYER 4 — ICS Integration
  Drone feed into EOC via ATAK / WebEOC
  Incident Action Plan updated with aerial intel
  Chief of Plans briefs Air Branch Director
